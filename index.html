Scrolling Through Change: How Major Political Events Reshape Instagram Language

This study uses machine learning to examine how language on Instagram changed surrounding the 2016 United States presidential election and whether these shifts are more detectable among political figures than influencers.

Introduction

Do our words change following major political events, even when we are not overtly discussing politics? In this study, we use machine learning to understand the politicization of social media platforms with natural language processing. We explore this by examining linguistic shifts within Instagram captions surrounding society-altering events. Specifically, we ask whether a large language model can differentiate between Instagram captions posted before versus after the 2016 United States presidential election, and if these language changes are more detectable for political figures than for influencers. We hypothesize that (1) the large language model can successfully distinguish when a caption was written relative to a major societal event, and (2) captions from political figures will be more accurately classified (i.e., pre-election vs. post-election) than captions from influencers. We intend to connect these linguistic shifts to broader shifts in beliefs, or how our perspectives change in response to the world around us, seeing as language and beliefs are inherently interrelated.

The problem we address is how social media perpetuates political ideas through language and whether that language changes in response to significant events like elections. If machine learning models can correctly categorize captions as occurring before or after an event, we take this as a sign of language change and, by extension, a change in latent beliefs. In an age where social media largely dictates public discourse, it is important that we understand how political ideas are communicated through language and which groups reinforce these ideas, be it politicians themselves or influencers who indirectly impact this public conversation.

This study is also of personal interest to us, given our academic foundations in psychology and our interest in political science. We chose the 2016 United States presidential election to investigate in particular because it was, to us, the catalyst for the distinctively polarizing political climate now present in American society. During the 2016 election period, there was a rise in political idolization and a surge of supporters sharing polarizing perspectives across platforms like Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok. We believe that if belief-related language changes exist, they would be most prominent surrounding a monumental event like the 2016 election; hence, we use it as a case study for our present analysis.

Background

Historical Context

The politicization of social media has its roots in the early 2000s. One of the first major instances of using the internet for political communication occurred during Howard Dean’s 2004 presidential campaign, when Meetup.com, a web tool for forming social groups, was used to organize supporters online (Wolf, 2004). This role expanded significantly amid Barack Obama’s 2008 presidential campaign. Obama and his team utilized platforms like Facebook and MySpace in ways that had not previously been seen in national politics, primarily to spread the Obama message and drive voter participation (Price, 2020). Since it was the first election where presidential and congressional candidates attempted to connect with voters through social networking platforms, the 2008 cycle was often called the “Facebook election” (Dutta & Fraser, 2008). These early campaigns proved that social media platforms could be a major tool for political communication.

Many researchers identify the 2016 election as the true turning point in the relationship between social media and political polarization. During this period, online political language became more informal, confrontational, and emotionally charged (Külz et al., 2023). This change was largely influenced by Donald Trump’s direct and populist communication style on Twitter. Prior research comparing the rhetoric of that election found that while Hillary Clinton’s style was structured and professional, Trump frequently used informal language with strong negative sentiment (Pérez-Curiel & Limón Naharro, 2019). Because so much attention has been given to how the 2016 election reshaped political language online, we expect these differences between pre-election and post-election captions to be detectable by our machine learning model, especially for political figures. At the same time, there is little research surrounding how influencer language changed during this same period. Since influencers were less politically prominent in 2016 than they are today, they provide an interesting comparison to politicians whose language is already strongly connected to political events.

Following 2016, research has increasingly focused on how platform design itself impacts political discourse. Recent studies examine how algorithms create “echo chambers” or “filter bubbles,” where users are repeatedly shown content that matches their existing views (Barrett et al., 2021; The Henry Jackson Society, 2024). This can lead to extreme polarization, as people with similar beliefs continue reinforcing one another instead of engaging with opposing perspectives. Instagram occupies a unique space in this conversation as it focuses on visual storytelling and personal branding rather than dense policy, as seen in campaigns like Lula da Silva's in Brazil (Sandes & Carriço Reis, 2025).

Finally, a core part of our work is the relationship between language and beliefs. We look specifically to the principle of linguistic relativity, often referred to as the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis. While somewhat controversial, this theory suggests that the language people use, to some extent, determines what they think and believe; the words and grammatical structures within language can subconsciously dictate how we interpret the world (Elsevier, n.d.). For example, one study discussed in a Stanford report found that a sentence such as “girls are as good as boys at math” can unintentionally reinforce stereotypes because the phrasing implies that math is a “male” standard that girls are merely matching (Shashkevich, 2019). This demonstrates how language can actively impact beliefs and perceptions. Given this relationship between language and cognition, we believe that if our model can detect shifts in the way people communicate, those shifts may also be representative of broader shifts in society’s latent beliefs and perspectives.

Related Work

Machine learning has a well-established history of being used to track how language changes over time. Prior work by William L. Hamilton et al. (2016) focused on using word embeddings to quantify how word meanings shifted over time by comparing usage across six historical corpora spanning four languages and two centuries. Their work demonstrated that changes in word meaning can be systematically measured using computational models. These results were further supported by later work, such as Kutuzov et al. (2018), a survey paper reviewing existing approaches for tracing temporal changes in lexical semantics using word embeddings. Rather than conducting a single experiment, the paper synthesized prior research and showed that there had been a rapid increase in studies using these models to examine language changes over time. Both studies established semantic shift detection as an emerging and growing area within natural language processing and helped establish the broader foundation for our work.

While Hamilton et al. (2016) and Kutuzov et al. (2018) focused on detecting changes in language, other research has examined how political discourse develops within social media environments. A study by Eytan Bakshy et al. (2015) analyzed large-scale behavioral data from Facebook users. The study measured exposure by analyzing content that user networks share, what appears in users’ feeds, and what users choose to engage with. Their findings suggested that social media platforms can limit exposure to ideologically diverse viewpoints, which reinforces our claim that existing beliefs may contribute to politically polarized discourse. Though this study did not specifically look at political figures and influencers, it showed that social media environments themselves shape how political ideas can be spread.

The prior work most similar to our study involves: 1) research that uses machine learning to detect language change, as well as 2) research on political discourse within social media. While these two areas of interest have been studied separately, our work attempts to combine them by applying machine learning to identify shifts in language surrounding a major political event while comparing differences between political figures and influencers on Instagram. This comparison between politicians and influencers is one of the more original aspects of our work, as there is relatively little research examining how influencer language may shift in response to political events. In addition, rather than only identifying changes in language, our study aims to interpret these shifts in relation to broader changes in political discourse and shifts in underlying beliefs following the 2016 election. Overall, our project is original in that it advances prior research by combining machine learning, social media language, and political discourse analysis into a single study comparing politicians and influencers surrounding the 2016 United States presidential election.

Methods and Experiments

Data Preparation

Our study used a dataset of Instagram captions that we manually compiled from the public accounts of both political figures and influencers. We collected a total of 1,000 captions from 50 individual accounts, including 25 politician accounts and 25 influencer accounts. For each account, we collected 20 captions, with 10 captions from the pre-election period and 10 captions from the post-election period. We purposely designated time windows for data collection to prevent short-term noise surrounding the election period from impacting analyses. We collected pre-election data from November 2014 to November 2015 and post-election data from November 2017 to November 2018. Overall, each entry in the dataset includes the account username, account type (politician or influencer), the individual’s name, the caption text, the caption date, and a binary label that indicates the timeframe (0 = pre-election, 1 = post-election).

To prepare the data for modeling, we removed duplicate entries, converted all text to lowercase, and removed URLs and extra spaces. We also conducted exploratory checks: we verified the total number of observations, confirmed the number of accounts, analyzed the average caption length, and checked the distribution of labels and account types. Finally, we split the data at the account level (by username), not the post level, to prevent the model from memorizing individual writing styles. The dataset was divided into 70% training, 10% validation, and 20% test sets.

Baseline Model

For our baseline, we implemented a model using TF-IDF vectorization (5,000 features) and a logistic regression classifier. TF-IDF converts raw texts into a numerical matrix by weighting words based on their importance within each caption; it emphasizes words that are unique to a specific caption while downplaying common words that appear across the entire dataset. We opted for this approach because TF-IDF accounts for word importance, while logistic regression is a simple and interpretable classification method. This baseline model established a strong starting point for us to compare the performance of our more complex deep learning model.

To test the second part of our hypothesis, whether captions from political figures are more accurately classified than those from influencers, we analyzed performance separately for each group. After training the baseline model on our full training set, we applied it to the test set and filtered the results by account type to compute independent performance metrics for influencers and politicians using the same trained model for a fair comparison. We evaluated results using the macro-F1 score, alongside accuracy, precision, and recall.

Baseline Ablation Experiments

Our ablation experiments for the baseline TF-IDF model sought to identify which specific words the model valued most when predicting whether a caption was posted before or after the election. Using the model’s learned coefficients, we extracted the top 25 features most strongly associated with pre-election captions and the top 25 features most strongly associated with post-election captions. We then tested three conditions: 1) removing only pre-associated words, 2) removing only post-associated words, and 3) removing all 50 keywords. We applied these changes across the training, validation, and test sets. For each condition, we retrained the TF-IDF vectorizer and logistic regression model to observe how the removal of these features affected model performance and overall reliance on these words.

Core Model

For our core analysis, we implemented a deep learning model using BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) to understand more complex semantic patterns in our Instagram captions. BERT uses contextual embeddings to learn the meanings and relationships within a sentence. It is especially useful in our case, as it can detect components like user intent and the context-heavy language found on social media. We fine-tuned a pretrained BERT-base-uncased model on our dataset to classify captions as pre-election (0) or post-election (1). Before training, the captions were tokenized and converted into input representations, with padding and truncation applied to a maximum length of 150 tokens. We trained the model for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 2e-5 and a batch size of 8. As with our baseline, we assessed performance using the macro-F1 score and accuracy. To test the second part of our hypothesis, we again analyzed model performance separately for influencer and political accounts using the same trained model.

Core Model Ablation Experiment

To analyze how our core model was making its predictions, we conducted an ablation experiment where we removed the most predictive keywords, identified by our TF-IDF baseline, from each caption in our dataset. We designed this experiment to determine whether BERT primarily learned from keywords or from contextual information and linguistic patterns within each caption. We applied this modification across our training, validation, and test sets to create an ablated version of our data. We then retrained the BERT-base-uncased model on this dataset using our original parameters, including 3 training epochs, a learning rate of 2e-5, and a batch size of 8. To assess the impact of the ablation, we compared the performance of the original and ablated models using macro-F1 scores and accuracy. We also examined differences in training behavior through loss curves, as well as changes in attention patterns, to see how removing keywords affected the model’s ability to learn and generalize across both political and influencer accounts.

Results

Baseline Model

The results of our baseline TF-IDF logistic regression model show initial performance on our classification task. Our primary metrics of success include macro-F1 scores and accuracy, which we report across all analyses. On the test set, the model obtained a macro-F1 score of 0.55, an accuracy of 0.55, a recall of 0.55, and a precision of 0.55. On the validation set, the model obtained a macro-F1 score of 0.61, which was higher than the test set performance. The classification report shows similar performance across both classes, with an F1 score of 0.56 for pre-election captions and 0.54 for post-election captions. The confusion matrix (Figure 1) demonstrates this distribution. Out of 100 pre-election captions, 57 were correctly classified, and 43 were not correctly classified; out of 100 post-election captions, 53 were correctly classified, and 47 were not correctly classified (Figure 1). We also observed a difference in performance between political figures and influencers. For political accounts, the model resulted in a higher F1 score of 0.59 and an accuracy of 0.59. For influencer accounts, the model resulted in an F1 score of 0.51 and an accuracy of 0.51

Baseline Model Ablation Experiments

Using the baseline TF-IDF logistic regression model, we identified the words most strongly associated with pre-election and post-election captions. The top pre-election words included “start,” “fox,” “do,” “excited,” “hillary,” “sayfie,” “ready,” “drive,” “guys,” and “cover.” The top post-election words included “kavanaugh,” “thank,” “christmas,” “link,” “trump,” “nation,” “now,” “out,” “tag,” and “was.” When words associated with pre-election categorization were removed, the model produced an F1 score of 0.62. Discussion

Our findings largely support our initial hypotheses, but they also revealed patterns we did not quite anticipate. Our first hypothesis, that a large language model could distinguish between captions from before and after the 2016 election, was best corroborated by the performance of our BERT model. BERT significantly outperformed our baseline, and notably, the ablated version of our BERT model (in which we removed keywords) performed best overall. As demonstrated in Figure 3, the confusion matrix for the ablated BERT model shows stronger classification performance, especially for post-election captions. These results suggest that there is a detectable distinction between pre-election and post-election language in Instagram captions. While this distinction is not extremely pronounced and the F1 scores are not perfect, it is consistent enough for a contextual model like BERT to identify. This indicates that major political events, such as the 2016 United States presidential election, may, in fact, leave a quantifiable imprint on everyday language, even in spaces like Instagram where content is not wholly political.

The most unexpected finding was that model performance improved during the ablation experiments. Specifically, when we removed the 50 keywords that were often politically related, such as “kavanaugh” and “hillary,” the model’s F1 score improved. This potentially means that the model initially overfit to these keywords, and once they were removed, it was forced to look at other features of the text, such as the tone and structure of the captions. This result supports our intuition that the 2016 election changed people’s language and how they expressed themselves online. The fact that the simpler TF-IDF baseline struggled while the contextual BERT model succeeded supports the idea that these changes are not surface-level but based on people’s tone, phrasing, context, among other factors.

Our second hypothesis, that these language changes would be more detectable in political figures than in influencers, was also firmly supported. Across all experiments, political account captions were consistently easier for the model to categorize. Figure 6 illustrates this pattern for both the original and ablated BERT models. Thus, while influencer language seemed relatively stable and less connected to the election cycle, political figures seemed to adopt a new linguistic norm following the election. To us, this suggests that there may have been at least some reshaping of public discourse among those more directly involved in political communication. This is understandable given that political figures’ captions are more tied to these events, whereas influencer captions traditionally remain within the world of lifestyle and personal topics. From this, we can take away that belief-related language shifts are more detectable in political discourse than in general social media content and, crucially, that not all language among all people responds equally to major political events.

We began this study with the notion that language and beliefs are interrelated. While we cannot access someone’s internal beliefs, language is an accessible means of observing how individuals interpret and respond to the world around them. The fact that our large language model could effectively categorize captions as pre-election and post-election, even when the most obvious, often politically related keywords were removed, indicates a change in how people communicate, which we believe is closely connected to changes in their latent beliefs. On a platform like Instagram, users do not always outwardly state their beliefs; however, beliefs can still surface indirectly through their tone, what they emphasize, or how they frame situations. In this sense, language becomes an indicator of how individuals and groups process major societal events. At the same time, we must recognize that language is not a perfect proxy for belief or thought. While our results suggest that belief-related shifts may be present, what we ultimately observe are patterns in communication, not exact measures of internal belief.

Limitations

While our results are promising, we acknowledge that our dataset is a primary limitation. Because the data was collected manually, our sample consists of 1,000 captions, which is a small amount for a machine learning task. Though we attempted to build a larger dataset via automated data scraping methods, we were met by challenges with Instagram’s private GraphQL API. We consistently encountered “edges” errors as Instagram updated its internal schema, such that tools like Instaloader no longer match its API format. As a result, this small sample size does increase the risk of overfitting, where the model may learn patterns specific to our 50 accounts rather than language shifts in the general public.

Another limitation of this study relates to how we interpret the “important” words identified by the baseline model. Many of these words were not obvious political terms but instead commonly used words such as “and,” “for,” or “is.” Hence, it is ambiguous whether the model is classifying based on these filler words instead of more interpretable and significant features. Due to this, we are limited in our ability to pinpoint the specific aspects of language the model is using to make its predictions.

Lastly, in this study, we focus only on Instagram captions. The type of language on Instagram is very different from the language on platforms like Twitter or Reddit, where users tend to be much more direct about political opinions. We intentionally chose Instagram as a platform for exploration because we wanted to focus on language more connected to daily life, with aspects of political language included, but not as the main focus. However, this also means that our findings may be specific to the Instagram platform and may not generalize to other, more political forms of social media.

Recommendations and Next Steps

Given three additional months, our first priority would be to expand our dataset, whether through additional manual collection or more advanced paid data scraping tools. A larger sample would have given the model more room to learn and help it recognize patterns that are more generalizable to the public. We would also extend this analysis beyond the 2016 election to include additional major political events, such as other election cycles. Further, it would be of interest to us to explore how language changes surrounding more socially impactful events, such as the COVID-19 global pandemic or social movements like #MeToo. This would help us determine whether some events lead to greater or more dramatic changes in belief-related communication than others. Lastly, it would be interesting to expand beyond the Instagram platform to platforms like Twitter or Reddit. These platforms vary in how users communicate (i.e., often more explicit political discourse), so we could compare how language changes across the platforms and determine whether the patterns we observed are universal or specific to Instagram.

Ethical Implications

There are important ethical considerations surrounding this study, especially regarding privacy and how we interpret what people post online. Although the data we collected consists of publicly accessible Instagram captions, most users do not post with the expectation that their words will be analyzed to predict their beliefs. In a larger-scale research setting, this type of work would likely require greater consideration of user consent and data use policies. Additionally, while our study shows that machine learning can be used to detect changes in everyday belief-related language, we must be cautious with respect to how we interpret these findings. As previously mentioned, language does not exactly equal belief; thus, interpreting someone’s caption as a fixed map of their internal perspective can be misleading and lead to unfair overgeneralizations. Therefore, machine learning methods such as these must be used responsibly to prevent possible targeting or harm to users.

Conclusion

Our study sought to answer two main questions: first, whether a machine learning model could differentiate between Instagram captions from before and after the 2016 election, and second, whether those linguistic changes would be more visible in politicians than in influencers. Our results supported both hypotheses. We found that the 2016 election cycle left a predictable mark on social media language, especially among those already engaged in the political world. The most compelling part of this project was the outcome of the ablation experiments. The fact that our model performed best after removing obvious keywords demonstrated that these changes in language are truly rooted in deeper changes in the tone and structure of how people write. This suggests that as our social and political climates shift, the means by which we express our experiences and communicate with others shift accordingly. Thus, our findings point toward real change in latent beliefs, reflected indirectly through the patterns of our everyday language.

Artificial intelligence was not completely necessary to observe that language changed, but it made a vast difference in what we were able to detect. Our baseline model showed that word importance alone could only tell us so much and was prone to overfitting to obvious keywords. In contrast, the BERT model was effective at moving beyond surface-level patterns. It appeared to pick up on the context and intent behind the captions, which brought us closer to our goal of understanding how shifts in communication related to changes in internal perspectives over time. In conclusion, we consider this project to be a success. We were able to directly address our central research questions and provide support for our hypotheses. More importantly, this project indicates that the language we use in our daily lives, even on a casual platform like Instagram, is not superficial. It reflects how we process and respond to major societal events. In demonstrating that these changes are detectable even without the most obvious political and societal words, we show that the 2016 election did more than change what we say, but reshaped how we express our ideas and communicate our lives to the world altogether.
