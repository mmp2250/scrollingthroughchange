Introduction

Do our words change following major political events, even when we are not overtly discussing politics? In this study, we use machine learning to understand the politicization of social media platforms with natural language processing. We explore this by examining linguistic shifts within Instagram captions surrounding society-altering events. Specifically, we ask whether a large language model can differentiate between Instagram captions posted before versus after the 2016 United States presidential election, and if these language changes are more detectable for political figures than for influencers. We hypothesize that (1) the large language model can successfully distinguish when a caption was written relative to a major societal event, and (2) captions from political figures will be more accurately classified (i.e., pre-election vs. post-election) than captions from influencers. We intend to connect these linguistic shifts to broader shifts in beliefs, or how our perspectives change in response to the world around us, seeing as language and beliefs are inherently interrelated.

The problem we address is how social media perpetuates political ideas through language, and whether that language changes in response to significant events like elections. If machine learning models can correctly categorize captions as occurring before or after an event, we take this as a sign of language change and, by extension, a change in latent beliefs. In an age where social media largely dictates public discourse, it is important that we understand how political ideas are communicated through language and which groups reinforce these ideas, be it politicians themselves or influencers who indirectly impact this public conversation.

This study is also of personal interest to us, given our academic foundations in psychology and our interest in political science. We chose the 2016 United States presidential election to investigate in particular because it was, to us, the catalyst for the distinctively polarizing political climate now present in American society. During the 2016 election period, there was a rise in political idolization and a surge of supporters sharing polarizing perspectives across platforms like Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok. We believe that if belief-related language changes exist, they would be most prominent surrounding a monumental event like the 2016 election; hence, we use it as a case study for our present analysis.

Background

Historical Context

The politicization of social media has its roots in the early 2000s. One of the first major instances of using the internet for political communication occurred during Howard Dean’s 2004 presidential campaign, when Meetup.com, a web tool for forming social groups, was used to organize supporters online (Wolf, 2004). This role expanded significantly amid Barack Obama’s 2008 presidential campaign. Obama and his team utilized platforms like Facebook and MySpace in ways that had not previously been seen in national politics, primarily to spread the Obama message and drive voter participation (Price, 2020). Since it was the first election where presidential and congressional candidates attempted to connect with voters through social networking platforms, the 2008 cycle was often called the “Facebook election” (Dutta & Fraser, 2008). These early campaigns proved that social media platforms could be a major tool for political communication.

Many researchers identify the 2016 election as the true turning point in the relationship between social media and political polarization. During this period, online political language became more informal, confrontational, and emotionally charged (Külz et al., 2023). This change was largely influenced by Donald Trump’s direct and populist communication style on Twitter. Prior research comparing the rhetoric of that election found that while Hillary Clinton’s style was structured and professional, Trump frequently used informal language with strong negative sentiment (Pérez-Curiel & Limón Naharro, 2019). Because so much attention has been given to how the 2016 election reshaped political language online, we expect these differences between pre-election and post-election captions to be detectable by our machine learning model, especially for political figures. At the same time, there is little research surrounding how influencer language changed during this same period. Since influencers were less politically prominent in 2016 than they are today, they provide an interesting comparison to politicians whose language is already strongly connected to political events.

Following 2016, research has increasingly focused on how platform design itself impacts political discourse. Recent studies examine how algorithms create “echo chambers” or “filter bubbles,” where users are repeatedly shown content that matches their existing views (Barrett et al., 2021; The Henry Jackson Society, 2024). This can lead to extreme polarization, as people with similar beliefs continue reinforcing one another instead of engaging with opposing perspectives. Instagram occupies a unique space in this conversation as it focuses on visual storytelling and personal branding rather than dense policy, as seen in campaigns like Lula da Silva's in Brazil (Sandes & Carriço Reis, 2025).

Finally, a core part of our work is the relationship between language and beliefs. We look specifically to the principle of linguistic relativity, often referred to as the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis. While somewhat controversial, this theory suggests that the language people use, to some extent, determines what they think and believe; the words and grammatical structures within language can subconsciously dictate how we interpret the world (Elsevier, n.d.). For example, one study discussed in a Stanford report found that a sentence such as “girls are as good as boys at math” can unintentionally reinforce stereotypes because the phrasing implies that math is a “male” standard that girls are merely matching (Shashkevich, 2019). This demonstrates how language can actively impact beliefs and perceptions. Given this relationship between language and cognition, we believe that if our model can detect shifts in the way people communicate, those shifts may also be representative of broader shifts in society’s latent beliefs and perspectives.

Related Work

Machine learning has a well-established history of being used to track how language changes over time. Prior work by William L. Hamilton et al. (2016) focused on using word embeddings to quantify how word meanings shifted over time by comparing usage across six historical corpora spanning four languages and two centuries. Their work demonstrated that changes in word meaning can be systematically measured using computational models. These results were further supported by later work, such as Kutuzov et al. (2018), a survey paper reviewing existing approaches for tracing temporal changes in lexical semantics using word embeddings. Rather than conducting a single experiment, the paper synthesized prior research and showed that there had been a rapid increase in studies using these models to examine language changes over time. Both studies established semantic shift detection as an emerging and growing area within natural language processing and helped establish the broader foundation for our work.

While Hamilton et al. (2016) and Kutuzov et al. (2018) focused on detecting changes in language, other research has examined how political discourse develops within social media environments. A study by Eytan Bakshy et al. (2015) analyzed large-scale behavioral data from Facebook users. The study measured exposure by analyzing content that user networks share, what appears in users’ feeds, and what users choose to engage with. Their findings suggested that social media platforms can limit exposure to ideologically diverse viewpoints, which reinforces our claim that existing beliefs may contribute to politically polarized discourse. Though this study did not specifically look at political figures and influencers, it showed that social media environments themselves shape how political ideas can be spread.

The prior work most similar to our study involves: 1) research that uses machine learning to detect language change, as well as 2) research on political discourse within social media. While these two areas of interest have been studied separately, our work attempts to combine them by applying machine learning to identify shifts in language surrounding a major political event while comparing differences between political figures and influencers on Instagram. This comparison between politicians and influencers is one of the more original aspects of our work, as there is relatively little research examining how influencer language may shift in response to political events. In addition, rather than only identifying changes in language, our study aims to interpret these shifts in relation to broader changes in political discourse and shifts in underlying beliefs following the 2016 election. Overall, our project is original in that it advances prior research by combining machine learning, social media language, and political discourse analysis into a single study comparing politicians and influencers surrounding the 2016 United States presidential election.
