Scrolling Through Change: How Major Societal Events Reshape Instagram Language

In this project, we ask: To what extent can a language model detect belief shifts by distinguishing between Instagram captions posted before versus after the 2016 United States presidential election, and does this detection accuracy differ between influencer and political figure accounts?

Introduction

Do our words change following major political events, even when we are not overtly discussing politics? In this study, we use machine learning to understand the politicization of social media platforms with natural language processing. We explore this by examining linguistic shifts within Instagram captions surrounding society-altering events. Specifically, we ask whether a large language model can differentiate between Instagram captions posted before versus after the 2016 United States presidential election, and if these language changes are more detectable for political figures than for influencers. We hypothesize that (1) the large language model can successfully distinguish when a caption was written relative to a major societal event, and (2) captions from political figures will be more accurately classified (i.e., pre-election vs. post-election) than captions from influencers. We intend to connect these linguistic shifts to broader shifts in beliefs, or how our perspectives change in response to the world around us, seeing as language and beliefs are inherently interrelated. The problem we address is how social media perpetuates political ideas through language, and whether that language changes in response to significant events like elections. If machine learning models can correctly categorize captions as occurring before or after an event, we take this as a sign of language change and, by extension, a change in latent beliefs. In an age where social media largely dictates public discourse, it is important that we understand how political ideas are communicated through language and which groups reinforce these ideas, be it politicians themselves or influencers who indirectly impact this public conversation. This study is also of personal interest to us, given our academic foundations in psychology and our interest in political science. We chose the 2016 United States presidential election to investigate in particular because it was, to us, the catalyst for the distinctively polarizing political climate now present in American society. During the 2016 election period, there was a rise in political idolization and a surge of supporters sharing polarizing perspectives across platforms like Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok. We believe that if belief-related language changes exist, they would be most prominent surrounding a monumental event like the 2016 election; hence, we use it as a case study for our present analysis.
